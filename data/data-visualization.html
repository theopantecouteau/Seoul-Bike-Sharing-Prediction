#%% 
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns
#%% 
df = pd.read_csv('./data/seoul-bike-data-clean-for-dataviz.csv')
df.drop(columns="Unnamed: 0", inplace=True)
#%% 
df.describe().T
#%% 
plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6))
sns.lineplot(data=df, x="Hour", y="Rented Bike Count", markers='o')
plt.title("Rented Bike Count by Hour")
plt.xlabel("Hour of the Day")
plt.ylabel("Rented Bike Count")
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()
#%% md 
We can see on the graph above that the rented bike count is at its lowest at 4am and at its highest at 6pm, with a local maximum at 8am and a local minimum at 2pm. 
In fact, the rented bike count is as its highest during the commuting hours, which is not surprising.

# 1. Rented Bike Count by Month
plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6))

plt.title("Rented Bike Count by Month")
plt.xlabel("Month")
plt.ylabel("Rented Bike Count")

plt.show()
#%% md 
We can see that the rented bike count is increasing from January to June, then decreasing from July to December. 
It seems consistent with the fact that the weather is getting warmer from January to June, then colder from July to December.

#loop on the column and display the boxplot
for col in df.columns:
    plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6))
    sns.boxplot(x=col, data=df)
    plt.title(f'{col} Distribution')
    plt.xlabel(col)
    plt.show()
#%% md 
This boxplot is interesting in our outlier analysis. We can see on some columns that there are some outliers, but they are not so far from the median. This confirms that our data is clean and our decision to keep the outliers was a good one.
